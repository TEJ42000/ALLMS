The Conflict: Can Customary Law Override Statutory Law?

ðŸ§  MEMORY: "J vs C" = "Julian = Custom Wins" vs "Constantine = Custom Loses"

âœ… JULIAN'S VIEW (Pro-Customary Law)

Time: 2nd century AD (Â±115-172 AD)
Emperor: Hadrian
Source: Digest (D. 1.3.32.1)

Argument: YES, customary law CAN derogate from statutory law

Reasoning:

  • Laws bind us because accepted by the people's judgment
  • What difference if people show will by vote OR by conduct?
  • Laws can be abrogated by the legislator's vote OR by disuse (silent consent)

KEY: People create law through action, not just voting!

âŒ CONSTANTINE'S VIEW (Anti-Customary Law)

Time: 4th century AD (319 AD)
Context: Imperial Rome (Emperor holds power)
Source: Codex Justinianus (C. 8.52.2)

Argument: NO, customary law CANNOT override statutory law

Reasoning:

  • Custom has authority, BUT limited
  • Custom cannot "contravene common sense or the law"
  • People transferred legislative power to Emperor (leges de imperio)
  • Once power transferred, people can't negate emperor's law

KEY: Emperor's law = supreme, custom must yield!
