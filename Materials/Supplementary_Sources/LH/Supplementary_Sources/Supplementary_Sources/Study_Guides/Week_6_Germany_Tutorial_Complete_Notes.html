ðŸ“‹ Defense Arguments at Nuremberg

1. Ex Post Facto Law Objection

  • "Crimes against peace" were not clearly defined before 1945
  • "Crimes against humanity" were completely new legal concepts
  • Defendants couldn't have known these were criminal acts
  • Violates fundamental legal principle: laws cannot be retroactive

2. "Victor's Justice" Critique

  • Winners judging losers (Allied powers judging Axis powers)
  • No prosecution of Allied war crimes (e.g., Dresden bombing, atomic bombs)
  • Tribunal composed entirely of Allied judges
  • Selective application of international law

3. Superior Orders Defense

  • "I was just following orders from superiors"
  • Military discipline requires obedience
  • Individual soldiers not responsible for policy decisions

3. Prosecution Counter-Arguments

Response to Ex Post Facto Objection

Argument 1: Declaratory, Not Constitutive Law

  • London Charter declared existing principles, didn't create new ones
  • Pre-existing customary international law prohibited these acts
  • Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928) outlawed aggressive war
  • Hague Conventions established war crime prohibitions
  • The Charter simply formalized what was already illegal
DECLARATORY vs. CONSTITUTIVE LAW: Constitutive Law (Creating New Law): [No previous law] â†’ [Charter creates law] â†’ [Prosecution] âŒ This would violate nullum crimen Declaratory Law (Declaring Existing Law): [Customary law exists] â†’ [Charter declares it] â†’ [Prosecution] âœ… This respects nullum crimen

Argument 2: Natural Law Supersedes Positive Law

  • Some acts are inherently criminal (murder, genocide)
  • Nazi actions violated fundamental human morality
  • No state can legalize atrocities through domestic law
  • Higher moral law transcends written legal codes

Argument 3: Necessity for Justice and Deterrence

  • Unprecedented atrocities require unprecedented response
  • Failure to prosecute would encourage future tyrants
  • Establishing accountability for international crimes
  • Justice for millions of victims demands prosecution

Response to Superior Orders Defense

London Charter Article 8:

"The fact that the Defendant acted pursuant to order of his Government or of a superior shall not free him from responsibility, but may be considered in mitigation of punishment."

Tribunal's Reasoning:

  • Moral Choice Remains: Individuals can refuse manifestly illegal orders
  • Knowledge of Wrongfulness: Defendants knew actions were atrocities
  • Command Responsibility: Leaders cannot hide behind hierarchy
  • Ordinary Soldier Exception: Low-level soldiers may have mitigation, but high commanders do not
SUPERIOR ORDERS - TWO TESTS: Test 1: Was the order MANIFESTLY ILLEGAL? 5. Limitations and Criticisms of Radbruch Formula

âš ï¸ CRITICAL ANALYSIS: Problems with Radbruch Approach

1. Vagueness of "Extreme" Threshold

  • No clear standard for when injustice becomes "extreme"
  • Subjective judgment by courts after the fact
  • Different judges might reach different conclusions
  • Creates unpredictability and undermines legal certainty

2. Still Arguably Retroactive

  • Guards couldn't have known in 1980s that their actions would later be deemed "non-law"
  • Radbruch Formula wasn't applied by GDR courts at the time
  • Guards reasonably believed they were following valid law
  • Formula applied retrospectively by different legal system (FRG)

3. Victor's Justice Concern

  • Similar to Nuremberg: winners judging losers
  • FRG legal system judging GDR legal system
  • Western liberal values imposed on former communist state
  • No prosecution of FRG officials for their actions

4. Selective Application

  • Other states use force at bordersâ€”why single out GDR?
  • Many unjust laws existâ€”which ones get Radbruch treatment?
  • Appears politically motivated rather than legally principled

Key Concepts You MUST Know

Essential Legal Principles

1. Nullum crimen sine lege / Nulla poena sine lege

  • Definition: No crime/punishment without pre-existing law
  • Why it matters: Protects against arbitrary prosecution
  • Tension: Conflicts with punishing extreme injustice

2. Radbruch Formula

  • Three parts: Conflict â†’ Severity assessment â†’ Validity denial
  • Threshold: Ordinary injustice (law valid) vs. Extreme injustice (law void)
  • Application: GDR Border Law = extreme injustice

3. Superior Orders Defense

  • Claim: "Just following orders"
  • Nuremberg: Rejected for manifestly illegal orders
  • Wall Shooters: Rejected using Radbruch (orders were "non-law")

4. Crimes Against Humanity vs. War Crimes

  • War crimes: Only during war; enemy targets
  • Crimes against humanity: Before/during war; any civilians; systematic

5. Natural Law vs. Legal Positivism

  • Positivism: Law = state enactment (validity separate from morality)
  • Natural law: Law must conform to moral principles
  • Both cases lean toward natural law to overcome nullum crimen

Sample Exam Questions & Approach

Question Type 1: Legal Principle Application

Q: "Did the Nuremberg Trials violate the principle of nullum crimen sine lege? Discuss."

Structure Your Answer:

  1. Define nullum crimen: Explain principle and its importance
  2. Present defense argument: London Charter = ex post facto law
  3. Present prosecution response:
    • Declaratory interpretation (existing customary law)
    • Natural law supersedes positive law
    • Justice necessity argument
  4. Analyze tension: Acknowledge legitimate concerns on both sides
  5. Conclude: Take position but recognize complexity

Question Type 2: Comparative Analysis

Q: "Compare the legal justifications used at Nuremberg with those in the Wall Shooters cases."

Structure Your Answer:

  1. Identify common problem: Both face nullum crimen challenge
  2. Nuremberg approach:
    • Natural law theory
    • Declaratory vs. constitutive law
    • Customary international law
  3. Wall Shooters approach:
    • Radbruch Formula
    • Extreme injustice exception
    • German constitutional principles
  4. Compare and contrast:
    • Similarities: Both invoke moral principles over positive law
    • Differences: International vs. domestic; scale; specificity
  5. Evaluate: Which approach is more legally defensible?

Question Type 3: Critical Evaluation

Q: "Evaluate the Radbruch Formula as a solution to the Wall Shooters dilemma."

Structure Your Answer:

  1. Explain Radbruch: Formula's three parts and logic
  2. Arguments FOR Radbruch:
    • Prevents absolute immunity for state crimes
    • Balances legal certainty with justice
    • Limited exception (only extreme cases)
    • Reflects universal moral principles
  3. Arguments AGAINST Radbruch:
    • Vague "extreme" threshold
    • Still arguably retroactive
    • Unpredictable application
    • Victor's justice concerns
  4. Alternative approaches: What else could Germany have done?
  5. Conclude: Overall assessment with nuance

THE BIG PICTURE

Central Theme: How can legal systems respond to extreme injustices committed under previous regimes while respecting fundamental legal principles like nullum crimen sine lege?

The Tension:

  • Legal Certainty: People must know what's criminal in advance
  • VS.
  • Justice/Accountability: Extreme crimes cannot go unpunished
